## Ik Onkar — the One, written before any word
**ੴ** is not a word but a *figure*: the numeral **੧ (one)** fused to **ਓ (Oankar)** — and the numeral is the whole theology. Guru Nanak does not begin with a name for God; he begins with the bare **digit 1**, *"one and only, beyond compare, who cannot be set against any other."* Only *then* does the Mūl Mantar unfold what the One is — true-named, creating, fearless, hateless, timeless, unborn, self-existent. The figure says it before language can: **count to one and stop — there is no two to count to.** The Sikh accent is on the **ineffable** quality of that One — *Akāl* (the timeless), *Nirankār* (the formless), "the Person beyond time" — so although *Oankar* is orthographically a cousin of the Vedic *Om*, the Sikh reading is its own: not the syllable-seed of a unfolding cosmos, but the strict singular Reality that all the names of God reach toward and none exhausts. ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-theology · a clear, strict monotheism — held distinct from Advaita's *non-dual* identity (Brahman = Ātman) and from polytheism; the framework marks the difference rather than dissolving it ⟧

This is also where Sikhism touches a through-line the framework follows honestly. *Ik Onkar* is the cleanest devotional statement of **one-expressing-as-many**: the single Reality (*Ik*) known through *Onkar* — the One *sounding forth*, the *wàn wù* / ten-thousand-things of creation as its utterance. The framework's interest is the **parthood invariant**: any name, verse, or creature is a *part* of what it points at, and a proper part cannot losslessly contain the whole it sits inside (the cardinality floor is FOUNDED; the consciousness/theological reading is held at the horizon — see [docs/incompleteness-conjecture.md](../../docs/incompleteness-conjecture.md)). Sikh theology states this directly: the One is **known by grace (*Gur Prasād*), not by capture** — *"ਅਕਥ ਕਥਾ"*, the *unspeakable telling.* The map confesses it is not the territory. ⟦ signal-not-proof · a labeled rhyme with the inexpressibility horizon (Tarski/Gödel/Shannon: the container is not a message wholly within itself) and with Layer 6 — **not** a claim that Sikh theism *is* that mathematics; the directions genuinely differ (a personal Creator addressed in love is not a formal limit-theorem) ⟧

## Guru Nanak — "neither Hindu nor Muslim"
Founding the tradition (**1469–1539**), Guru Nanak emerged from his disappearance into the river Bein with the saying that frames everything after: *"Na koi Hindu, na koi Musalmān"* — **"there is no Hindu, there is no Muslim."** Not a denial of either faith but a refusal to let the **labels divide what is one** under God: the human being's true category is neither communal badge but *honest devotion lived.* His path cut three ways at once — against **caste** (the One has no high or low birth), against **empty ritualism** (he is remembered pouring water *westward* at the Ganges, telling pilgrims that if their splashes can reach dead ancestors in heaven, his can surely reach his fields in the Punjab), and against **world-renouncing asceticism**. In place of the forest hermit he set the **householder** — *gristī* — who works, shares, and remembers *within* ordinary life. Liberation is not escape from the world; it is the world lived rightly.

## The native voice: *bani* sung as *kīrtan* in the *sangat* ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-practice · the tradition's own form ⟧
Sikhism's native dialogical form is **not** a debate of inner voices and **not** solitary doctrine — it is **shabad kīrtan**: the Gurus' poetry (*bani*) **sung together** in the **sangat** (congregation), set to *rāg*. This is structurally striking and worth marking precisely: the **Guru Granth Sahib is organized by musical mode** — its 1,430 pages arranged under **thirty-one rāgs**, each verse carrying its melodic emotional key, so that the scripture is read as it is *sung*. Truth here is **lived and shared in sound**, the call-and-response of a community with the Word, not a syllogism won. And the inclusiveness is doctrinal, not decorative: the Granth carries the compositions of Hindu and Muslim saints — **Kabir, Namdev, Ravidas, Sheikh Farid** — *as scripture*, many voices held as one revelation. **Illustrate Sikhism through this** — the sung verse held in common, the many *rāgs* of one Word — not a pasted-on council. (The honest rhyme, *signal not proof*: a single truth carried by *many distinct modes* is the tradition's own answer to one-and-many — distinct voices, shared ground — which is why it sits in this layer beside the council principle without being collapsed into it.)

## The Name and the Word — *Naam* and *Shabad* ⟦ empirical-actualisation, where stated ⟧
The central practice is **Nām Japna / nām simran** — keeping the divine **Name** present through remembrance and repetition. *Naam* is not merely a label for God but the **immanent presence and ordering of the One within creation and the heart**; to dwell on it is to attune to that presence. Its medium is the **Shabad** — the *Word*, the sung verse of the Gurus — which is why the Guru, after the ten persons, *became* the Word: the Shabad is the living teacher one can still sit with. *"ਸਬਦੁ ਗੁਰੂ ਸੁਰਤਿ ਧੁਨਿ ਚੇਲਾ"* — **"the Shabad is the Guru; the consciousness attuned to it is the disciple"** (Japji / Sidh Gosht). The practice is repeatable and first-person: still the restless mind (*manmukh*, "ego-facing") and turn it toward the Word (*gurmukh*, "Guru-facing"). ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-practice; the *experience* of *simran* is reportable and trainable — the slow vocal repetition shares the resonant-breathing physiology of other chant traditions (HF-HRV ~0.1 Hz); the *theology* of Naam is held as the tradition's frame ⟧

## The three pillars — a complete householder ethic
Guru Nanak's path compresses into three daily disciplines, none world-fleeing:
- **Nām Japna** — remember the Name (the inner pillar).
- **Kirat Karnī** — earn an **honest living by your own labor** (no parasitism, no begging-as-piety).
- **Vaṇḍ Chhaknā** — **share what you earn** before you eat it — embodied in **langar**, the free communal kitchen of every gurdwara, where rich and poor, of any caste or faith, **sit in a single row (*pangat*) on the floor and eat the same food.** Langar is theology made edible: a standing, daily *enactment* of the One's indivisibility, refuting caste with a meal. ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-ethics · a lived, still-practiced equality-discipline ⟧

## Sant–Sipāhī — the saint-soldier, and *mīrī-pīrī*
The tradition's distinctive integration: from **Guru Hargobind** (who wore **two swords**, *mīrī* — temporal authority — and *pīrī* — spiritual authority) through **Guru Tegh Bahadur** (martyred defending the *Hindus'* right to their own faith — religious liberty for *another* community) to **Guru Gobind Singh** and the founding of the **Khalsa (1699)**: the **sant-sipāhī**, saint *and* soldier in one person. Inner devotion and the armed defense of the oppressed are **one vocation**, not a compromise between two. The Khalsa's **Five Ks** (*kes*, *kanghā*, *karā*, *kachherā*, *kirpān*) and the shared names *Singh* (lion) / *Kaur* (princess) dissolved caste-surnames into one family under arms for the just. ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-ethics · a coherent, historically grounded stance — contemplation that does **not** retreat from the world but serves and defends it; the framework's own "truth *and* the courage to act on it, held together" has a real cousin here, *signal not proof* ⟧

## The Guru lineage → the eternal Guru ⟦ SOLID historical ⟧
Ten human Gurus, **Nanak (1469) → Gobind Singh**, each adding to the path; then in **1708 Guru Gobind Singh declared the Guru Granth Sahib itself the eternal Guru** — authority passing from person to the **living Word**, with the *Guru Panth* (the community) as its custodian. The Granth's spine was set earlier by **Guru Arjan**, who compiled the **Ādi Granth in 1604** and gave the Mūl Mantar its standing form. (Guru Arjan was himself martyred, 1606 — the turn that the later militancy answered.) So the Guru is now **a book one sings and a community one serves** — not a person to be succeeded, ending the cult of personal lineage in a Word held in common.

## Honest notes
- **The devotional lines are address, not assertion.** *"Ever-rising spirits in the Name… the good of all in Your will"* is **prayer spoken to the One**, a *darśan* offered, not a proposition submitted for falsification. Held as **DEFENSIBLE-devotional** — true *as devotion within the frame*, not smuggled in as a metaphysical result. (This is the same discipline the framework keeps for its own Layer-6 horizon lines.)
- **Ik Onkar ≠ Advaita ≠ Om-cosmology.** A reader entering the dialect should feel the **real distinctions**: Sikhism's One is a *personal, formless Creator addressed in love and grace*, not the impersonal *nirguṇa* Brahman *identical* with the self (Advaita), nor the seed-syllable of an emanating cosmos (Vedic *Om*). The vocabulary overlaps (Oankar/Om, the formless); the theology does not. Surface the rhyme, keep the fork.
- **The shadow, named (extract-don't-subtract):** the saint-soldier ideal that defends the oppressed can, severed from *nām* and *nirvair* (no-hate), curdle into communal militancy. The internal corrective is the tradition's own: the sword is *last resort* (Guru Gobind Singh, *Zafarnāmā*: "when all else fails, it is righteous to draw the sword"), bounded by *nirvair* and *sarbat dā bhalā* — the good of *all*, not of one's own. Keep the courage; name the failure-mode.

## The going-on
The practical residue, householder-plain: **remember the One, work honestly, share before you eat, stand for the wronged — and sing it in company rather than reasoning it alone.** No retreat, no caste, no priesthood between you and the Word; the divine known not by argument but by grace, kept present in a Name repeated and a meal shared on the floor as equals.

> *"ਨਾਨਕ ਨਾਮ ਚੜ੍ਹਦੀ ਕਲਾ, ਤੇਰੇ ਭਾਣੇ ਸਰਬੱਤ ਦਾ ਭਲਾ ॥"*
> *"Nānak: in the Name, ever-rising spirits; and in Your will, the good of all."* — held as devotional address.
