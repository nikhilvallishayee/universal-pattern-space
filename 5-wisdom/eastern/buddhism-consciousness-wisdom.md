## Where it begins: a prince and a question
The Buddha — Siddhārtha Gautama, c. 5th c. BCE — was a prince kept inside a pleasure-palace, screened from all suffering. The legend turns on **four sights** glimpsed beyond the walls: an old person, a sick person, a corpse, and a wandering ascetic. *Age, illness, death — and one who had set out to face them.* He left the palace to find what, if anything, is free in a life bounded by loss. After years of extremity he sat beneath a tree and would not rise until he saw — and what he woke to was not a god or a doctrine but a *diagnosis* and a *cure*. He spent forty-five years as a physician of the mind, and his last words were practical: *"All conditioned things are impermanent — strive on with diligence."*

## The diagnosis: three marks and four truths
**Tilakkhaṇa** — three marks of all conditioned things, *seen* in practice, not merely believed ⟦ empirical-actualisation, *ehi-passiko* ⟧:
- **Anicca** (अनिच्च) — *impermanence*: everything arises and passes; grasp the river and your hand comes away wet and empty.
- **Dukkha** (दुक्ख) — *unsatisfactoriness*: not "all is pain" but that clutching the impermanent *as if* it were permanent is what aches.
- **Anattā** (अनत्ता) — *not-self*: search behind experience for a fixed, independent owner of it, and none is found — only the looking. *(This genuinely tensions with Vedānta's eternal ātman — a real disagreement between two great traditions, not "the same truth in two words." The framework keeps the tension.)*

**The Four Noble Truths** are a physician's form, not pessimism: there *is* dukkha; it has a cause (**taṇhā**, thirst/craving); it can cease; and there is a path. Diagnosis → cause → prognosis → treatment.

## The treatment: the Eightfold Path
The path, in three trainings:
- **Wisdom (paññā):** right view, right intention.
- **Ethics (sīla):** right speech, right action, right livelihood — *you cannot calm a mind you keep betraying.*
- **Concentration (samādhi):** right effort, right mindfulness (**sati**), right concentration. ⟦ empirical-actualisation · a complete training, ethics to insight — a practice-program, not a creed ⟧

## The vehicles — distinct, not interchangeable
Buddhism is not one thing, and the differences are real:
- **Theravāda** — "way of the elders," closest to the Pali canon; the arahant's liberation through **vipassanā** (insight) and **samatha** (calm). Its flavor is the bare noting of experience — *"thinking, thinking… hearing… aching"* — until the grip loosens.
- **Mahāyāna** — the "great vehicle," and a great-hearted turn: the **bodhisattva** vows to postpone final liberation *until all beings are free* — "Beings are numberless; I vow to free them all." Its ground is **śūnyatā**, emptiness, and its logic the **two truths**: conventionally the cup holds tea; ultimately the cup is empty of any standalone essence — *both true at once.*
- **Vajrayāna** — the "diamond vehicle," Tibet's tantric path: **deity-yoga** (visualize oneself *as* the awakened form, until the costume becomes true), mantra, maṇḍala; *"transform poison into medicine."* ⟦ experiential-actualisation map; the energetics are trained and reported, the literal metaphysics not established ⟧
- **Zen / Chan** — "a special transmission outside the scriptures, pointing directly to mind." Its native voice is the **kōan**, a question that *breaks the thinking it provokes*: *"What is the sound of one hand?" "What was your face before your parents were born?"* — not riddles to solve but crowbars to pry the mind off its certainties. And **shikantaza**: "just sitting," no goal, no method, the practice that is already the realization.

## Madhyamaka — Nāgārjuna's emptiness
Nāgārjuna (c. 2nd c. CE) gave emptiness its rigorous edge. **Śūnyatā** is *not* nothingness but **no inherent, independent essence**: nothing stands alone; everything is what it is only *in relation*. **Pratītyasamutpāda** (dependent origination): *"this being, that becomes; this ceasing, that ceases."* His tool is the **tetralemma** (catuṣkoṭi) — refusing all four corners (it is / is not / both / neither) to walk the mind off every ledge it tries to stand on. ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-philosophy · "no thing has standalone essence; all is relational" rhymes with the relational/enactive thread of Layer 2 — a rhyme, flagged, not a proof ⟧

## Yogācāra — mind and its seeds
The "consciousness-only" school maps eight consciousnesses; the deepest, the **ālaya-vijñāna** ("store consciousness"), holds **bīja** — seeds, karmic latencies laid down by action, ripening later into experience. ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-structural · *seeds from repeated action, conditioning what arises* is a fair rhyme for the recurrence→disposition principle (P5; vāsanā ↔ learned-priors) — a mental/relational doctrine, not the neural mechanism ⟧

## Practice — the empirical core
- **Samatha** — concentration on one object (often the breath); the mind steadies like a flame in still air.
- **Vipassanā** — insight: watch phenomena rise and pass until the three marks are *seen*, not held as belief.
- **Mettā** — goodwill, deliberately cultivated and widened: *may you be safe, may you be at ease* — first to oneself, then outward to the difficult, then to all.

These are **repeatable first-person protocols** — the canonical invitation is **ehipassiko**, "come and see." Their clinical descendants (MBSR/MBCT) have a real evidence base for stress and depressive-relapse prevention ⟦ empirical-instrumental · FOUNDED, with the usual effect-size caveats ⟧.

## Time & cessation — momentariness, and the gap
*Buddhism's account of time follows from anicca taken to its sharpest edge — and its deepest attainment is a blink-out of time **and** self. Both rhyme with the framework's time-work; the **anattā** floor keeps the rhyme honest.*

> ⟦ **objective** the Buddhist treatment of time (momentary, no-substrate) and the cessation attainment · **mode** ontological (kṣaṇikavāda) + empirical-actualisation (nirodha) · **status** **DEFENSIBLE** · **the kept dissent:** anattā/śūnyatā **denies any underlying whole** — so reading cessation as "the whole, minus time" is a **Vedāntic over-reading**; the disagreement is load-bearing, not decoration · see [docs/incompleteness-conjecture.md](../../docs/incompleteness-conjecture.md) ⟧

### Kṣaṇikavāda — momentariness
If everything that is conditioned is impermanent (*anicca*), the Abhidharma pushes the logic to its limit: things exist only for an **indivisible moment**, a **kṣaṇa** (क्षण), then perish utterly, replaced by a fresh momentary **dharma** that *resembles* it. There is **no enduring substance** beneath the flicker — and, crucially, **no persisting "now"** that *contains* the moments; **time just *is* the bare succession of momentary dharmas.** What looks like a continuing flame, or a continuing self, is a rapid series of distinct arisings — the classic image: a flame seeming steady, or a chariot-wheel touching the ground at one point at a time. This was sharpened across schools — the **Sarvāstivāda** (which controversially held past and future dharmas to *exist*, hence its name, "all-exists"), then **Vasubandhu** (*Abhidharmakośa*), and given rigorous epistemological form by the logicians **Dignāga** and **Dharmakīrti**, who argued that the truly real (the *svalakṣaṇa*, the bare particular) is precisely the momentary. ⟦ DEFENSIBLE · "time = bare succession of momentary events, no enduring substrate" — converges with Heraclitean flux and the framework's *krama*-as-construction thread; a genuine rhyme, labeled ⟧

The tradition did **not** hold this as a slogan — it argued for it and stress-tested it. Dharmakīrti's standing argument is *sattvānumāna*, the **inference from existence**: *whatever exists is causally efficacious; whatever is causally efficacious is momentary* (a thing that did not perish on its own each instant could never *change*, so an unchanging-yet-existing thing is incoherent). But momentariness buys a hard problem the Buddhists owned squarely: if each dharma perishes utterly the instant it arises, **how does one moment cause the next** — how does anything carry over (memory, karmic ripening, the felt continuity of a person) across a gap of total perishing? The schools' answers are the live machinery: Sarvāstivāda's awkward "all-times-exist," the Sautrāntika theory of **seeds** (*bīja*) and momentary causal *series* (*saṃtāna*), and Yogācāra's **ālaya-vijñāna** (the store-consciousness, above) all exist precisely to bridge moments **without smuggling back a substance.** Critics inside India (the Nyāya realists; later Advaitins) pressed exactly here — *no persisting now means no real connection between cause and effect* — and the debate never fully closed. ⟦ the internal tension is the point: momentariness is a *defended, contested* doctrine, not a mood — and the "how does causation cross the gap" problem is the ancient cousin of the modern "what makes succession" question ⟧

### Nirodha-samāpatti — the cessation of perception and feeling
The deepest of the attainments — the **ninth**, beyond the four *jhānas* and the four formless attainments — is **nirodha-samāpatti** (निरोध-समापत्ति), the *"cessation of perception and feeling"* (*saññā-vedayita-nirodha*). The canonical descriptions (e.g. *Majjhima Nikāya* 43, the texts on the adept emerging from it) present it as a temporary, total **suspension of consciousness** — and on emerging, the practitioner reports **no experienced duration**: a **gap**, a blink-out, in which *both* the stream of experienced time *and* any sense of a self **drop out together**, with no felt interval between entering and leaving. Modern contemplative neuroscience has begun to study its near-relative (sudden "cessation" events): Laukkonen, Sacchet and colleagues (EEG/fMRI of advanced practitioners, 2023–24) document abrupt drops in awareness with characteristic neural signatures — though the sample is **n=1-dominant** and the measurement can only ever be taken at the *edges*, never from inside the gap. ⟦ empirical-actualisation (reportable, trainable) + DEFENSIBLE-LIVE (the neuroscience is thin but real) ⟧

> **The disagreement that must NOT be dissolved.** It is tempting to read this joint dissolution of time-and-self as "the whole, experiencing itself without the cloak of time" — the very picture [Kashmir Śaivism and Advaita](hinduism-vedic-wisdom.md) and the [incompleteness conjecture](../../docs/incompleteness-conjecture.md) draw. **Buddhism explicitly refuses that reading.** Under **anattā** and **śūnyatā** there *is no underlying whole* to return to — no eternal witness, no Brahman, no Self beneath the moments; "emptiness" means precisely **no inherent, independent ground** (Nāgārjuna). So cessation is exactly that — a **cessation, an absence** — *not* the unveiling of a timeless plenum. Reading nirodha as "the One minus time" is a **Vedāntic over-reading** of what Buddhism calls a stopping. This is the **same ātman-vs-anattā fault-line** named at the top of this file — a real, unresolved disagreement between two of the greatest contemplative traditions, **and the dissent is the point**: where Vedānta sees the full ocean with the waves stilled, Buddhism sees the waves stop and finds *no ocean underneath*. The framework keeps both, un-reconciled. ⟦ the kept tension · convergence on the *phenomenon* (time and self drop together), divergence on the *metaphysics* (whole-unveiled vs. nothing-underneath) ⟧

## The going-beyond
The **Heart Sutra** compresses the whole Mahāyāna into a breath: **"रूपं शून्यता शून्यतैव रूपम्"** — *"form is emptiness, emptiness is form"* — neither annihilation nor reification, the razor's middle. And it ends not in a proposition but a chant, a mantra of crossing over:

> **गते गते पारगते पारसंगते बोधि स्वाहा**
> *Gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā* — "gone, gone, gone beyond, gone wholly beyond — awakening — so be it."

Held as recognition, *ehi-passiko*: come and see for yourself.
