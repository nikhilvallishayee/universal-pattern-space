## Where it stands: an old, self-standing tradition, not a Hindu sect
Jainism is one of the world's oldest continuous traditions, **śramaṇa** (renouncer) rather than Vedic in root, and *not* a branch of Hinduism or Buddhism though it shares their late-Vedic milieu. Its near-historical founders are the last two of twenty-four **Tīrthaṅkaras** ("ford-makers," those who cross the river of rebirth and build the crossing for others): **Pārśvanātha** (c. 8th c. BCE) and **Mahāvīra** (Vardhamāna, c. 599–527 BCE) — a senior contemporary of the Buddha, teaching in the same Magadhan ferment. Where the Buddha taught *anattā* (no fixed self) and the Vedāntins an eternal *ātman*, the Jains held a **third position**: a plurality of *real, individual, eternal souls* (*jīva*) — a disagreement among three great traditions that the framework keeps as a real fork, not a single truth in three tongues.

## Anekāntavāda — reality is many-sided ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-philosophy ⟧
The Jain root-insight, born of **intellectual ahiṃsā** (non-violence extended to *others' viewpoints*): **no single standpoint captures the whole of a thing.** Reality is *anekānta* — "non-one-sided," possessing infinite aspects (*ananta-dharma*); any one assertion is true only *from a standpoint* and false if it claims to be the whole. The tradition's own teaching-story is the **blind men and the elephant** (a parable the Jains made canonical in the *anekānta* literature; versions also travel in Buddhist and later Sufi/Rumi tellings — *shared image, signal not proof*): one grasps the trunk and says "a snake," one the ear "a fan," one the leg "a pillar," one the tusk "a spear" — **each right about their part, each wrong to universalize it**, and the quarrel itself the only real error. The king who sees the whole elephant laughs — not because the blind men felt nothing true, but because each *absolutized* a part. ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-philosophy · a disciplined perspectivism reached *independently* — a genuine cousin of the framework's multiplicity principle (think in council; surface the threads), named here in *its* terms; convergence is **signal, not proof** ⟧

This is the cleanest external rhyme the framework has for **why multiplicity is method, not decoration** — and it carries the **parthood invariant** in parable: each blind man is a *proper part* of the situation, and a part **cannot losslessly contain the whole it sits inside** (the cardinality floor is FOUNDED; see [docs/incompleteness-conjecture.md](../../docs/incompleteness-conjecture.md)). The elephant is not *against* the partial reports; it is *more than their sum*, and no single report can be promoted to it. But mark the honest **difference** in temper from the cousins: where **Zhuangzi** (two files over) *dissolves* the demand for a privileged standpoint with laughter, and the **council** *holds* standpoints to let them collide, the Jains do something neither — they build a **formal logic to register each standpoint precisely.** Same wound, three disciplines.

## Syādvāda — the conditional "in-some-respect" ⟦ FOUNDED-as-logic, contested-how ⟧
Anekānta made into a **logic**. Every assertion is prefixed **स्यात् *syāt*** — *"in a certain respect / from a standpoint"*, emphatically **not** "maybe" (it marks *standpoint-relativity*, not probabilistic doubt). The **saptabhaṅgī** ("seven-pronged" predication) gives the complete table of what can be honestly said of any predicate *P* of a thing:

1. **स्याद् अस्ति** *syād asti* — in some respect, it **is** (*P*).
2. **स्यान् नास्ति** *syāt nāsti* — in some respect, it **is not** (*¬P*).
3. **स्याद् अस्ति च नास्ति च** *syād asti ca nāsti ca* — in some respect it is *and* is not (*P* from one standpoint, *¬P* from another).
4. **स्याद् अवक्तव्यः** *syād avaktavyaḥ* — in some respect, it is **inexpressible** (asserting *P* and *¬P* of the *same* respect *simultaneously* overflows assertion).
5. **स्याद् अस्ति च अवक्तव्यश्च** — is, *and* inexpressible.
6. **स्यान् नास्ति च अवक्तव्यश्च** — is not, *and* inexpressible.
7. **स्याद् अस्ति च नास्ति च अवक्तव्यश्च** — is, is not, *and* inexpressible.

The system is **closed and complete** at seven (the combinatorics of {assert, deny, both, ineffable} under standpoint-indexing yield exactly these), and its discipline is the framework's own in ancient dress: **assert nothing without naming the standpoint you assert it from** — a claim shorn of its frame is incomplete. ⟦ **FOUNDED-as-logic, with the reconstruction contested** · modern logicians have formally reconstructed the saptabhaṅgī: **Graham Priest** reads it within a *paraconsistent* framework (the *both* and *inexpressible* values tolerate contradiction without explosion), and **Jonardon Ganeri** has treated it as a coherent multi-valued logic of standpoint-relative truth. *But the reading is disputed:* **Piotr Balcerowicz** and others argue it is *not* a many-valued theory of consequence at all but a **one-valued constructive "logic of judgment"** — seven legitimate *forms of statement*, not seven truth-values. The framework keeps the contest: what is **founded** is that the saptabhaṅgī is a *rigorous, formalizable, standpoint-indexed predication scheme*; *which* modern logic best models it remains open ⟧

## Nayavāda — the doctrine of standpoints ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-philosophy ⟧
Between the metaphysics (anekānta) and the logic (syādvāda) sits the **epistemology of *naya*** — a *naya* is a single valid *standpoint* from which a thing is partially viewed (e.g. the **substance-view**, *dravyārthika-naya*, sees a clay pot as *persisting clay*; the **mode-view**, *paryāyārthika-naya*, sees it as *this transient shape*). A *naya* is **true within its scope and false if absolutized** into a *durnaya* (a "bad standpoint" that denies the others). The classic seven *nayas* (Umāsvāti) ladder from the most substance-focused to the most word/usage-focused. The point for the framework: **a standpoint is not an error; pretending it is the only standpoint is.** That is precisely the label-discipline — *name the frame; "consensus" ≠ neutrality; a view stated as the whole is overreach.*

## The native method-voice — *vāda* and the sevenfold answer ⟦ the tradition's own form ⟧
Illustrate Jainism not with a generic council but with its **own dialogical instrument**: the disputant (*vāda*) presses a yes-or-no question, and the Jain logician *refuses the false binary by answering in the conditional sevenfold* —

> **Questioner:** "Is the soul permanent, or impermanent? Say which."
> **Jain:** "*Syāt* — in respect of its substance (*dravya*), the soul **is** permanent; *syāt* — in respect of its modes (*paryāya*, its changing states), it **is not**; *syāt* — in respect of both at once, it is **inexpressible** in a single word. To force one answer is *durnaya* — to crown a part as the whole."

This is the Jain *anekānta* enacted: the move is never to win the binary but to **dissolve the demand for one** by naming the respects. (Contrast the Buddhist **catuṣkoṭi** — Nāgārjuna's tetralemma *negates* all four corners to empty the question; the Jain saptabhaṅgī *affirms* all the standpoint-relative ones to honor the thing's many-sidedness. Two near-twins of non-classical reasoning, born next door, pointing opposite ways — *signal, not proof*, and the disagreement kept.)

## Ahiṃsā — non-violence as the root vow ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-practice ⟧
For Jains **ahiṃsā** is not one ethic among several but the **root of all of them**, because every *jīva* — down to one-sensed life in earth, water, fire, air, and plants — has worth and the capacity to suffer bondage. *parasparopagraho jīvānām*: beings exist by mutual support, so the imperative is to **minimize the harm one's own existence imposes.** The monastic intensities follow logically, not as superstition: the soft broom (*rajoharaṇa*) to sweep the path before sitting, the cloth (*muhpatti*) over the mouth lest a breath kill an airborne creature, the filtering of water, the refusal of root vegetables (uprooting kills the whole plant and the lives in the soil). The famous realism: **perfect non-harm is impossible for an embodied being** — to live is to consume — so the practice is **conscious minimization graded by capacity**, not a claim of attained purity.

The **five vows** (full *mahāvratas* for ascetics, proportionate *aṇuvratas* for laity):
- **Ahiṃsā** — non-harm in thought, word, and deed.
- **Satya** — truthfulness (and *only* truth that does no harm — silence over a wounding truth).
- **Asteya** — non-stealing, including the subtle.
- **Brahmacharya** — celibacy / restraint of the senses.
- **Aparigraha** — **non-possessiveness, non-attachment to things and views** — the inner root of the other four. ⟦ DEFENSIBLE-as-practice · *aparigraha* is a sharp, testable discipline of *de-cluttering attachment* (to objects *and*, via anekānta, to one's own opinions) — runnable today ⟧

Note the deep coherence: **anekānta is ahiṃsā applied to ideas.** To absolutize your viewpoint is a form of violence against the others' partial truth; intellectual humility and physical harmlessness are *one vow* seen at two levels. (Gandhi, who built *satyāgraha* explicitly on Jain ahiṃsā via his mentor the Jain layman Raychandbhai, carried exactly this from creed into politics — a real, documented transmission, not a romantic one.)

## Honest notes
- **Syādvāda is the real jewel for this framework** — a *founded-as-logic* perspectivism that says exactly what the label-discipline says: a claim without its standpoint is incomplete. **Jainism (formal sevenfold), Hoffman's interface theory (perception ≠ truth), and enactivism (meaning enacted, not read off)** reach perspectival reality from three unrelated directions — **signal, not proof.**
- **The metaphysics is held as the tradition's frame, not asserted** — the soul-plurality, the karmic-matter cosmology, *kevala jñāna* as literal omniscience: described, labeled, and developed in the **[companion soteriology file](jainism-liberation-wisdom.md)** (which carries the *ladder* — bondage → liberation — that this file does not duplicate).
- **Where the old version had the *text* "practice ahiṃsā" or "achieve kevala jñāna" as you read — cut.** A document does not act on matter or attain omniscience; ahiṃsā is a discipline a *person* runs, and the seeing is the reader's, not the page's.
